## What Is Simple Linear Regression?

Simple linear regression models the relationship between **one independent variable (x)** and **one dependent variable (y)** by fitting a straight line through the data. The word "simple" distinguishes it from *multiple* linear regression, which uses two or more predictors.

The fitted line is chosen to minimize the sum of squared vertical distances between each data point and the line — a method called **ordinary least squares (OLS)**. The result is an equation of the form:

**y = b₀ + b₁x**

Where **b₀** is the y-intercept, **b₁** is the slope, and together they define the best-fit line.

### When Should You Use It?

Use simple linear regression when:

- You have **one continuous predictor** and **one continuous outcome**
- A scatter plot of your data shows an **approximately linear pattern**
- You want to **predict** y from x, or **quantify** how much y changes per unit of x
- Your sample size is large enough to yield stable estimates (n ≥ 10 is a common rule of thumb)

### When Should You Avoid It?

Avoid it when:

- The scatter plot shows a **clear curve** — try quadratic regression instead
- You have **multiple predictors** that jointly influence y — use multiple regression
- Your data contains **extreme outliers** that could pull the line away from the true trend
- The variables are **categorical** rather than numeric

## The Dataset

Throughout this guide, we'll work with a concrete example. Suppose a tutoring company tracks how many hours each student studies before a standardized test and their resulting score:

| Student | Study Hours (x) | Test Score (y) |
|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1       | 2               | 65             |
| 2       | 4               | 75             |
| 3       | 6               | 80             |
| 4       | 8               | 90             |
| 5       | 10              | 95             |

Five students, five data points. We'll compute the regression line that best predicts test score from study hours.

---

## Step 1: Calculate the Means

The first step is to compute the arithmetic mean of both variables.

**Mean of x (x̄):**

x̄ = (2 + 4 + 6 + 8 + 10) / 5 = 30 / 5 = **6.0**

**Mean of y (ȳ):**

ȳ = (65 + 75 + 80 + 90 + 95) / 5 = 405 / 5 = **81.0**

The means are the balancing points of each variable. The regression line will always pass through the point (x̄, ȳ) = (6.0, 81.0).

---

## Step 2: Compute the Deviations from the Mean

For each data point, calculate how far x and y deviate from their respective means:

| Student | x  | y  | x − x̄ | y − ȳ |
|---------|----|----|--------|--------|
| 1       | 2  | 65 | −4     | −16    |
| 2       | 4  | 75 | −2     | −6     |
| 3       | 6  | 80 | 0      | −1     |
| 4       | 8  | 90 | 2      | 9      |
| 5       | 10 | 95 | 4      | 14     |

Deviations tell you whether each observation is above or below average. Notice that Student 3 is exactly at the mean for x (0 deviation) and very close to the mean for y.

---

## Step 3: Calculate the Cross-Products and Squared Deviations

Now multiply the deviations to get the **cross-products** (x − x̄)(y − ȳ) and square each deviation to get **(x − x̄)²** and **(y − ȳ)²**:

| Student | x − x̄ | y − ȳ | (x − x̄)(y − ȳ) | (x − x̄)² | (y − ȳ)² |
|---------|--------|--------|-----------------|----------|----------|
| 1       | −4     | −16    | 64              | 16       | 256      |
| 2       | −2     | −6     | 12              | 4        | 36       |
| 3       | 0      | −1     | 0               | 0        | 1        |
| 4       | 2      | 9      | 18              | 4        | 81       |
| 5       | 4      | 14     | 56              | 16       | 196      |

The cross-products capture the **co-movement** of x and y: when both deviations are positive (or both negative), the product is positive, indicating they move in the same direction. The squared deviations measure the **spread** of each variable around its mean.

---

## Step 4: Sum Everything

Add up the columns:

**Σ(x − x̄)(y − ȳ)** = 64 + 12 + 0 + 18 + 56 = **150**

**Σ(x − x̄)²** = 16 + 4 + 0 + 4 + 16 = **40**

**Σ(y − ȳ)²** = 256 + 36 + 1 + 81 + 196 = **570**

These three sums are the building blocks of every regression statistic.

---

## Step 5: Calculate the Slope (b₁)

The slope tells you how much y changes for each one-unit increase in x. The formula is:

**b₁ = Σ(x − x̄)(y − ȳ) / Σ(x − x̄)²**

Plugging in our sums:

b₁ = 150 / 40 = **3.75**

**Interpretation:** For every additional hour of study, the predicted test score increases by **3.75 points**. This is a substantive effect — studying matters.

---

## Step 6: Calculate the Intercept (b₀)

The intercept is where the regression line crosses the y-axis (when x = 0). The formula uses the fact that the line passes through (x̄, ȳ):

**b₀ = ȳ − b₁ × x̄**

b₀ = 81.0 − 3.75 × 6.0 = 81.0 − 22.5 = **58.5**

**Interpretation:** A student who studies zero hours would be predicted to score **58.5** on the test. This is the baseline — the predicted outcome with no study time. Whether this is meaningful depends on context; a score of 58.5 at zero hours is plausible for a standardized test.

---

## Step 7: Write the Regression Equation

Combining the intercept and slope:

**y = 58.5 + 3.75x**

Or equivalently: **ŷ = 58.5 + 3.75x** (using the hat notation to indicate predicted values).

This equation is your model. Plug in any study time x and you get a predicted test score. For example:

- x = 3 hours → ŷ = 58.5 + 3.75(3) = 69.75
- x = 7 hours → ŷ = 58.5 + 3.75(7) = 84.75
- x = 12 hours → ŷ = 58.5 + 3.75(12) = 103.5

<Callout type="warning" title="Extrapolation Danger">
That last prediction (103.5 at 12 hours) exceeds the maximum possible test score if the test is out of 100. Predicting outside your data's range is called **extrapolation**, and it's the number-one source of absurd regression predictions. Always stay within the observed range of x unless you have domain knowledge justifying the extension.
</Callout>

---

## Step 8: Compute R-Squared (R²)

R² measures how much of the variation in y is explained by the model. First we need the **sum of squared residuals (SSres)** — the total squared difference between observed y and predicted ŷ:

| Student | x  | y  | ŷ     | y − ŷ | (y − ŷ)² |
|---------|----|----|-------|-------|----------|
| 1       | 2  | 65 | 66.0  | −1.0  | 1.00     |
| 2       | 4  | 75 | 73.5  | 1.5   | 2.25     |
| 3       | 6  | 80 | 81.0  | −1.0  | 1.00     |
| 4       | 8  | 90 | 88.5  | 1.5   | 2.25     |
| 5       | 10 | 95 | 96.0  | −1.0  | 1.00     |

**SSres** = 1.00 + 2.25 + 1.00 + 2.25 + 1.00 = **7.5**

**SStot** = Σ(y − ȳ)² = 570 (computed in Step 4)

**R² = 1 − SSres / SStot = 1 − 7.5 / 570 = 1 − 0.01316 = 0.9868**

**Interpretation:** The model explains **98.68%** of the variation in test scores. That's an exceptionally good fit — study hours account for nearly all the variability in performance for this dataset.

R² ranges from 0 (the model explains nothing) to 1 (the model explains everything). In real-world social-science data, R² values above 0.5 are typically considered strong; above 0.8 is very strong.

---

## Step 9: Calculate the Correlation Coefficient (r)

The Pearson correlation coefficient measures the strength and direction of the **linear** relationship:

**r = Σ(x − x̄)(y − ȳ) / √[Σ(x − x̄)² × Σ(y − ȳ)²]**

r = 150 / √(40 × 570) = 150 / √22800 = 150 / 151.0 = **0.9934**

The relationship between r and R² is straightforward:

**R² = r² = 0.9934² ≈ 0.9868**

This confirms our R² calculation. The correlation of +0.9934 indicates a **very strong positive linear relationship** — as study hours increase, test scores increase in an almost perfectly linear fashion.

### Interpreting the Correlation Scale

| |r| Range | Strength       |
|-----------|----------------|
| ≥ 0.9     | Very Strong    |
| 0.7–0.9   | Strong         |
| 0.5–0.7   | Moderate       |
| 0.3–0.5   | Weak           |
| < 0.3     | Very Weak/None |

Our r = 0.99 falls firmly in the "Very Strong" category.

---

## Step 10: Check the Assumptions

Before trusting your regression results, verify that the data satisfies the four OLS assumptions:

### 1. Linearity

The relationship between x and y should be approximately linear. Check with a scatter plot — if the points follow a straight-line pattern, you're good. Curved patterns suggest polynomial or transformed models.

### 2. Independence of Errors

Residuals should not be correlated with each other. This is mainly a concern for time-series data. If your observations are independent (as they typically are in cross-sectional studies), this assumption is usually met.

### 3. Homoscedasticity (Constant Variance)

The spread of residuals should be roughly the same across all predicted values. If the residuals fan out or funnel in, you have heteroscedasticity, which inflates standard errors and undermines significance tests.

### 4. Normality of Residuals

The residuals should be approximately normally distributed. This matters most for small samples (n < 30). With large samples, the Central Limit Theorem provides robustness.

For our 5-point dataset, formal testing is unreliable due to the tiny sample size, but the residuals (−1.0, 1.5, −1.0, 1.5, −1.0) are small and symmetric around zero — a good sign.

## The Complete Summary

For our study-hours vs. test-score example:

| Statistic          | Value    |
|--------------------|----------|
| Slope (b₁)         | 3.75     |
| Intercept (b₀)     | 58.5     |
| Regression Equation | y = 58.5 + 3.75x |
| R²                 | 0.9868   |
| Correlation (r)     | 0.9934   |

Every number tells a story: the slope says "studying pays off at 3.75 points per hour"; the intercept says "even with zero study, a baseline of 58.5 is expected"; R² says "your model captures 98.7% of what's going on"; and r says "the linear relationship is nearly perfect."

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Confusing Correlation with Causation

A high r value does **not** prove that x *causes* y. Study hours and test scores are correlated, but the causation runs through a third variable: the student's effort and motivation. Always pair regression with domain knowledge before making causal claims.

### Ignoring Outliers

A single extreme data point can dramatically shift the regression line. Always plot your data first, investigate outliers, and consider robust methods if outliers are legitimate but influential.

### Extrapolating Beyond the Data

Your regression model is only valid within the range of x values you observed. Predicting outside that range — especially far outside — can produce nonsensical results, as we saw with the 12-hour study prediction exceeding a 100-point maximum.

### Over-Relying on R²

A high R² doesn't mean your model is correct. You can have a high R² with a non-linear relationship if the curve is monotonic. Always inspect residual plots: if the residuals show a pattern (U-shape, funnel), the linear model is misspecified despite a high R².

### Using Regression for Categorical Data

Simple linear regression requires both variables to be continuous. If your predictor is categorical (e.g., male/female, red/blue/green), use a t-test or ANOVA instead — or encode the categories and use multiple regression with dummy variables.

---

## When to Move Beyond Simple Linear Regression

Simple linear regression is powerful, but it has limits. Consider these upgrades when:

- **You have multiple predictors** — salary depends on education, experience, industry, and location. Use **multiple linear regression** (y = b₀ + b₁x₁ + b₂x₂ + …) to model them jointly.
- **The relationship is curved** — diminishing returns, peak effects, or U-shaped patterns require **quadratic regression** (y = ax² + bx + c) or higher-order polynomials.
- **Your outcome is binary** — pass/fail, yes/no, click/don't-click. Use **logistic regression**, which models the probability of belonging to a category.
- **Your data has repeated measurements** — the same subjects measured over time. Use **mixed-effects models** with random intercepts for subjects.

Each of these extends the core idea of "fit a model to minimize prediction error" to a more complex data structure.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Simple linear regression fits a straight line** y = b₀ + b₁x that minimizes the sum of squared residuals.
2. **The slope b₁** equals the change in y per unit change in x; **the intercept b₀** is the predicted y when x = 0.
3. **R²** tells you the proportion of variance in y explained by x; **r** tells you the strength and direction of the linear relationship.
4. **Always verify assumptions** (linearity, independence, homoscedasticity, normality) before interpreting results.
5. **Correlation ≠ causation** — a strong statistical relationship doesn't prove that one variable causes the other.
6. **Never extrapolate** beyond the range of your data without domain justification.

The beauty of simple linear regression is that every calculation can be done by hand with just addition, multiplication, and division — as we've done here. Once you understand the mechanics, you can scale up to multiple predictors, polynomial terms, and logistic outcomes with confidence.
